Beginning Your First Assignment

As per the basic description on canvas, it is a good idea to include sketches and examples of previous work that inspired/influenced you. You need to explain what your site will look like, what it means, why you want to make it, how you will make it, and who has informed your ideas and approach.

In terms of the website itself, it does not have to be a functional commercial website, although if you have your heart set on it, you may. Think of your website as a proof of concept where the basic programming tools we are learning are the method of presenting the material/ideas/visual experience you are exploring. Your website may even be about these tools themselves.

Here are some ideas to get you thinking: You may wish to explore the formal qualities of html through experimenting with its default characteristics. What sort of experience does an overwhelming array of hyperlinks produce? Can you create a visual representation of a computational abstractions such as the cloud?

You may wish to create an abstract and/or purely aesthetic experience. How might you simulate death and reincarnation in the browser? How would you show how deep the ocean is? What would it mean to lucid dream inside the browser?—your imagination is the only limit! Here is a list of ideas to get you started. You will be marked on how your website and the experience it creates brings your ideas to life through an interactive experience, and how appropriately you have used the programming languages we will cover to do so.

This course will involve self-directed learning and research that is contingent upon on your specific idea and approach. It is not possible to teach or learn every single tag and attribute, and so you will need to go beyond what is taught in class to realise your projects. Getting in the habit of doing this research using freely available resources is a valuable skill, and is part of what you will learn. Today and over the next few weeks we will be using the W3 web tutorials, for instance. Do not be discouraged if it takes hard work to realise your idea; I will advise you if I think it is not achievable in the time we have available.

Note that programming is often difficult and time consuming—there will likely be moments in this course where you may feel frustrated or struggle to join the dots. DO NOT WORRY ABOUT THIS— together, and in some cases on your own, we can usually find out what we need to complete our assignments.

Brutalist Websites

There have been many trends in web-design, a relatively recent trend was for 'brutalist' websites (a term with its origins in modernist architecture). The use of this modernist term is instructive in that brutalist websites are brutally functional in the manner of, say, a Le Corbusier building, or perhaps a soviet apartment block. Form follows function, they are unadorned, they make use of only a bare minimum of materials, and so on. Brutalist websites often involve the unadulterated use, or even celebration, of the generic aesthetics of html itself—this site you are looking at now is an example of this design ethos. Think of geocities, craigslist, or some of these other examples below. As you can see below, Pascal Deville who coined the term clearly thinks the trend has passed!

Single Use Websites

There is an interesting genre of site often referred to as 'single use websites'. They are often strange, or a bit weird/ humorous, but can also be functional or interactive in a very specific way. You will notice that many internet artworks are essentially single use websites without a coherent or practical 'use'. What ideas do you have for an interesting single use website?

  • See how many people are in space right now.
  • Find out how secure your password is.
  • See all flights currently in the air. Perhaps not technically single use but you get the idea.
  • This site gives us a to-scale representation of the solar-system if the moon was the size of a single pixel
  • Single use websites can be a bit silly.

Internet art/Net.art

There is a canon of interesting art that is both of and about the architecture of the web—in both an aesthetic and a philosophical sense. Art that uses the formal and technical constraints of the internet as its medium has a partiular history. You're highly encouraged to look through this site and get a sense of this lineage. An interview with some of the key practitioners can be found here. A more critical historical appraisal can be found here.

There is too much to summarise here but we will look at a few seminal examples from the early period of internet art, often referred to as the 'net.art' period.

  • Russian artist Alexei Shulgin's Form Art is a seminal piece of net.art. Open it and have a play. Read more about it here.
  • He is also well known for his curatorial project Desktop Is which explored the desktop as a site of life, work and play.

You will notice that some if these works look 'old'. Shulgin's work is a good example of this; in the two images above you can see the work as it first appeared in 1997 via the Netscape browser, and how it appears today adapted to current browsers. Changes in screen resolution, the design of browsers and hardware and so on make it look quite different, although the code and underlying architecture is the same. What this reveals is that the fundamental architecture of the internet has not changed since Tim Berners Lee invented Hyper Text Transfer Protocol (http) and HTML (hyper text mark up language) in 1989. A website made back then can be viewed in more or less the same way now (although differences in browsers may prevent this).

Although there have been numerous design trends, and alternative web-authoring software has come and gone (flash for instance), the basis of almost all webpages remains HTML, CSS and Javascript. We are here to learn about this fundamental underlying architecture. We are learning to use these three fundamental coding languages, so that in the future you will be able to apply them to your own web-based work and projects. In order to do this, we will create a website that is formally and conceptually engaging, and that contains interactive elements. I am not expecting you to produce websites that appear 'advanced', 'professional' or commercially viable (of course if that is important to you that is okay too, but it is not our core concern).

  • JODI are another important example you will come across if you research net.art. Their website is in and of itself an interesting work. Read about their IDN series of works here.
  • Their IDN works use the formalist architecture of the web and the 'refresh' command to create endless loops. See one of them here. You can see the source code for this site below. Indeed, you can right click and 'view source' on almost any page.

Narrative Approaches

The narrative possibilities suggested by hyperlinks have been used by many artists and designers to construct interactive websites. The 'choose your own adventure' style website is a common trope. What can you do with it? What kind of story can you tell?

  • Olia Lialina is another well know internet artist who's work takes narrative form. This work presents the user with a seemingly endless series of narrative options.
  • Anna Anthropy's work Queers at the end of the World uses similar formal devices, but incorporates interactive elements to keep the user moving and simulate some of the anxiety and tension relationships sometimes induce.
